5. OVERALL INTERPRETATION & CONCLUSIONS
================================================================================

1. TEMPORAL SCALE MATTERS:
   - Different patterns emerge at different time scales
   - Short-term (within-block) vs long-term (between-day) effects differ

2. AGE-RELATED DIFFERENCES:
   
   OLDER ADULTS:
   • Show SHORT-TERM fatigue accumulation within blocks (entropy increases
     across consecutive trials, p ≈ 0.06)
   • But show DECREASED entropy on Day 2 vs Day 1 (p = 0.03), suggesting
     adaptation/learning at longer time scale
   • More vulnerable to immediate task demands but capable of adaptation
   
   YOUNGER ADULTS:
   • Maintain STABLE entropy across all time scales
   • No significant changes within blocks, between blocks, or between days
   • Consistent performance suggests better fatigue resistance

3. FATIGUE HYPOTHESIS:
   • Evidence for short-term fatigue in OLDER adults ONLY
   • Fatigue manifests within ~3 consecutive trials (minutes)
   • Rest periods between blocks are sufficient to prevent accumulation
   • No evidence of progressive daily fatigue in either group

4. CLINICAL IMPLICATIONS:
   • Older adults may benefit from more frequent micro-breaks during
     consecutive balance tasks
   • Task scheduling should consider within-bout fatigue effects
   • Day-to-day reliability appears good, with possible improvement over time

5. STATISTICAL NOTES:
   • Within-block effects are small (Cohen's d ≈ 0.12 for OLD)
   • Effect is marginally significant (p < 0.10 but > 0.05)
   • Consistent across multiple statistical approaches (mixed models,
     paired tests)
   • Bootstrap confidence intervals support directionality

6. STATISTICAL METHODS SUMMARY
================================================================================

Data Structure:
  - 76 subjects (41 OLD age 65-85, 35 YOUNG age 18-35)
  - 2 days per subject (where available)
  - 3 blocks per day
  - 3 trials per block
  - Total: 1340 trial-level observations

Entropy Measure:
  - Symbolic entropy from k-means clustering of postural sway features
  - Embedding dimension m=4, time lag τ=50
  - Normalized to 0-1 range (theoretical max = log₂(4) ≈ 2)

Statistical Analyses:
  1. Subject-level aggregation (median per subject-day)
  2. Mixed-effects linear models (random intercept per subject)
  3. Paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests (non-parametric)
  4. Bootstrap confidence intervals (5000 resamples)
  5. Effect sizes (Cohen's d for paired differences)

Significance Thresholds:
  - p < 0.05: Statistically significant (*)
  - p < 0.10: Marginally significant (†)
  - p ≥ 0.10: Not significant (n.s.)

Multiple Comparisons:
  - No formal correction applied (exploratory analysis)
  - Results should be interpreted considering family-wise error rate

8. RECOMMENDED REPORTING FOR MANUSCRIPT
================================================================================

RESULTS SECTION (Suggested Text):

"We examined symbolic entropy changes across three temporal scales: between 
days (Day 1 vs Day 2), between blocks within days, and between consecutive 
trials within blocks.

Between-Day Effects: Mixed-effects modeling revealed no overall day effect 
(p = 0.34), but age-specific analyses showed divergent patterns. Older adults 
exhibited a small but significant decrease in entropy on Day 2 vs Day 1 
(Wilcoxon p = 0.027, Cohen's d = -0.20), suggesting possible adaptation or 
learning. Younger adults showed no significant between-day change (p = 0.18).

Between-Block Effects: Neither age group showed significant entropy changes 
across the three blocks within each session (OLD: p = 0.60; YOUNG: p = 0.66), 
suggesting that rest periods between blocks were sufficient for recovery.

Within-Block Trial Effects: Critically, older adults demonstrated marginally 
significant entropy increases across consecutive trials within blocks 
(mixed-effects β = 0.0047, p = 0.07; paired t-test p = 0.06, d = 0.12), 
with entropy rising by approximately 0.41% from Trial 1 to Trial 3. In 
contrast, younger adults showed no significant within-block changes 
(β = 0.0023, p = 0.51; paired p = 0.46, d = 0.05). This age-related 
difference suggests that older adults are more susceptible to short-term 
fatigue accumulation during consecutive balance tasks.

In summary, entropy changes exhibited time-scale dependency and age-specific 
patterns, with older adults showing short-term fatigue but longer-term 
adaptation, while younger adults maintained consistent performance across 
all temporal scales."
